# Momentum First — Flow and Readability Guide

Status: working guide
Created: 2026-03-30
Revised: 2026-04-26
Purpose: support readable, disciplined manuscript prose across the current book structure without forcing one rhythm onto every part.

This guide is for drafting and review. It is not a mechanical lint standard, and cleanup/commit sessions should not enforce it by rewriting prose unless explicitly asked. Cleanup sessions may flag clear drift from the guide as a comment or follow-up item.

## Why this guide exists

The book's main prose risk is not lack of ideas. It is that precision, caution, and internal framework discipline can make the prose heavier than the reader's path requires.

The guide exists to prevent three recurring failures:
- body prose drifting into author-memo or framework-governance language,
- caution and status language overwhelming the claim being made,
- chapters becoming locally clear but monotonous or over-managed in sequence.

The goal is not to make the book casual. The goal is to make it legible at high conceptual density.

---

## Core principle

Write to the reader's path of understanding, not to the writer's path of composition.

That means:
- present the object, claim, pressure, or question early,
- let the prose build conceptual need before adding technical machinery,
- put limitations where they clarify the claim rather than where they pre-defend it,
- let flow arise from dependency and contrast, not from constant announcements.

---

## I. Current part-level style contracts

### Front matter / Preface

The Preface may use a more personal and reflective register than the numbered parts.

It should:
- preserve the personal origin and long-lived conceptual tension,
- move from human entry point to disciplined method,
- keep AI collaboration and verification language plain rather than apologetic,
- avoid becoming a second Introduction or a compressed technical chapter.

The Preface may include narrative pacing, short dramatic questions, and reflective transitions that would be too loose for Foundations.

### Part I — Introduction

The Introduction should be respectful, pressure-building, and non-overclaiming.

It should:
- begin from the success of existing physics, not from complaint,
- expose conceptual pressure without sounding like standard physics has failed,
- state the momentum-first wager clearly,
- explain how the book should be judged,
- prepare the reader for standards of evidence without turning into internal policy.

The Introduction can be more rhetorical than Foundations, but its rhetoric should build the reader's problem, not market the framework.

### Part II — Foundations

Foundations should sound firmer, leaner, and more declarative.

It should:
- fix terms and notation cleanly,
- distinguish postulate, realized working form, mapping, interpretation, and correspondence,
- use equations and figures as load-bearing structure,
- state scope and regime clearly without dragging the prose into anxiety,
- make downstream engines legible without trying to build them yet.

Foundations may use explicit signposting where dependency order matters. A line such as “the next section” or “this will matter later” is acceptable when it marks a real conceptual dependency.

## II. Sentence-level guidance

### 1. Put the topic early

Readers should quickly know what the sentence is about.

Prefer:
- `The realized split defines two positive directional quantities...`

Avoid defaulting to:
- `What the framework wants to say here is that...`
- `The point of this formulation is that...`

These phrases are not banned; they are signals to check whether the sentence can state the claim more directly.

### 2. Put new or important information late

The end of the sentence is a natural emphasis position.

Prefer:
- old/familiar material at the beginning,
- new/decisive material at the end.

This is a useful default, not a rigid formula.

### 3. One main action per sentence

A sentence may qualify or elaborate, but it should not perform several major jobs at once.

If a sentence introduces a concept, defends it, limits it, and points forward, it usually needs to be split.

### 4. End on substance when the sentence matters

Important sentences should not trail off into vague abstractions unless the abstraction is itself the point.

Watch endings such as:
- `matters`,
- `framework`,
- `structure`,
- `interpretation`,
- `context`.

They are sometimes right. They should not become the default resting place for important claims.

---

## III. Paragraph-level guidance

### 5. The first sentence should do real work

A paragraph should usually begin by defining the object, stating the claim, naming the problem, or carrying forward the previous paragraph's consequence.

Scene-setting is allowed when the transition itself matters, especially in Introduction and Preface. It should not become a way to postpone the claim.

### 6. One paragraph, one dominant job

Each paragraph should primarily do one of these:
- define,
- derive,
- interpret,
- qualify,
- transition,
- recap,
- build pressure.

A paragraph can move between these roles, but it should not drift from theory into policy note into caveat into transition unless that movement is deliberate and controlled.

### 7. Match opening and landing

A paragraph should usually end in the same conceptual key in which it begins.

If it opens as a definition, it should end by locking or cashing out that definition. If it opens as a qualification, it should end by sharpening the boundary it introduced.

Do not open in theory and end in editorial administration unless the paragraph's job is explicitly methodological.

### 8. Use old-to-new sequencing for local flow

When possible, begin from what the reader already has and land on the next piece of information. This creates flow without repeated verbal signposting.

## IV. Chapter/section guidance

The current book structure uses relatively small rendered chapter files for Introduction, Foundations, and Gravity. The Preface is the one approved wrapper chapter with included section files.

So the relevant unit is often a **chapter file**, not always a subsection.

A strong chapter file should answer:
1. What is the object, pressure, or claim here?
2. Why does it belong at this point in the reading order?
3. What does the reader gain from it?
4. What is its status, if that status is not obvious?

These answers do not need to appear as explicit headings or formulaic sentences. The rule is structural, not theatrical.

### Start with content, not process

Default opening order:
1. object/claim/pressure,
2. explanation,
3. limitation or status if needed.

Avoid the default reverse order:
1. author-process commentary,
2. status disclaimer,
3. eventual claim.

### End with payoff, boundary, or need

A chapter or section should ideally end by doing one of the following:
- locking in a result,
- sharpening a consequence,
- setting a boundary,
- exposing the next necessary question.

Forward pointers are acceptable when they mark real dependency order. They should not replace payoff.

### Vary rhetorical texture

Across a part, not every chapter should have the same shape.

Useful chapter textures include:
- respect/pressure building,
- wager statement,
- definition-led exposition,
- equation-led derivation,
- interpretive clarification,
- boundary/status summary.

Monotony is a real risk in technical prose. Variation should come from the job of the chapter, not decorative flourish.

## V. Qualification and status guidance

### Qualify sharply, not repeatedly

When a claim needs limitation, give the sharpest necessary limitation and then let the prose move.

Repeated nearby variants of the same caution make the book sound anxious rather than disciplined.

### Compress internal governance language

Claim-role and regime distinctions are essential. But in reader-facing prose they should usually be expressed naturally rather than as internal policy labels.

Prefer:
- one clear sentence,
- a compact boundary paragraph,
- or a status note where the reader genuinely needs it.

Avoid making ordinary body prose sound like a project-management document.

### Do not pre-defend every possible misreading

Default order:
1. state the claim,
2. explain it,
3. add the most relevant limit.

Only add more defensive clarification when the risk of misreading is real and local.

## VI. Signposting guidance

Signposting is useful when there is:
- a conceptual pivot,
- a regime shift,
- a change in claim type,
- a non-obvious inferential jump,
- a transition between major parts,
- or a dependency-heavy handoff.

Signposting is weak when it merely announces that a section exists.

Caution-flag phrases include:
- `The point is...`,
- `What matters is...`,
- `This should not be read as...`,
- `At this stage...`,
- `The next section...`,
- `This will matter later...`.

These phrases are allowed. If they appear often, check whether the prose is leaning on announcement instead of dependency.

---

## VII. Equation and figure guidance

### Equations must carry rhetorical weight

Every displayed equation should have a clear local function:
- define,
- formalize,
- derive,
- connect,
- or conclude.

If an equation does not change the reader's understanding, it probably belongs elsewhere.

### Surround equations with only necessary prose

Before or after an equation, prose should answer only the most relevant questions:
- what is this,
- what does it do,
- why is it here now,
- what status does it have?

Do not paraphrase the same equation several times unless each pass adds something new.

### Figures should clarify structure

A figure should show a relation, distinction, process, or dependency that prose alone would make heavier.

The surrounding text should tell the reader what structural relation to look for. Do not merely restate the caption.

---

## VIII. Review checklist

Before calling manuscript prose review-ready, ask:

1. Does the file reach its main object, pressure, or claim early enough for its part-level role?
2. Does each paragraph have one dominant job?
3. Is flow carried mostly by dependency, contrast, or old-to-new sequencing rather than repeated announcements?
4. Are caveats sharp and non-repetitive?
5. Do equations and figures carry real explanatory weight?
6. Does the file end with payoff, boundary, or a necessary next question?
7. Does the prose sound like manuscript prose rather than internal author notes?
8. Does the prose match the part-level style contract: Preface, Introduction, Foundations, or later engine chapter?

If two or more answers are no, revise before treating the file as ready.

---

## IX. Cleanup/commit boundary

Cleanup and commit sessions should not uphold this guide by rewriting style.

They may flag:
- obvious spelling or naming errors in canonical terminology,
- headings that no longer match the file's job,
- duplicated or stale copied prose,
- body prose that visibly reads like internal notes,
- equations or figures that appear to have no local function,
- repeated caveats that obscure rather than clarify.

Any substantive style revision should be an explicit writing/review task, not a side effect of cleanup.
